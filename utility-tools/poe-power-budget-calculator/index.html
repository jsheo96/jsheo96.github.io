Example

A switch with a 120 W PoE budget and a 10% safety reserve has 108 W of usable power. Eight devices drawing 12 W each, with a 5% allowance, require about 100.8 W, leaving 7.2 W of operating headroom.

Why use a reserve?

Startup current, heater activation, infrared LEDs, firmware changes, and device replacements can raise real power consumption above the normal average. A reserve helps prevent random device shutdowns or ports refusing to power on.

PoE standards and practical planning

The total switch budget is separate from the maximum available on each port. A switch may support high power on a single port but still run out of total budget when many ports are active. Always verify both the per-port class and the total budget.

FAQ

Should I enter typical or maximum device power?

Use the maximum rated input power for reliable planning. Typical draw is useful only for monitoring an already deployed system.

Does cable length reduce the switch's budget?

Cable resistance creates losses between the switch and the powered device. The calculator's allowance gives a simple way to include that effect, but exact loss depends on cable gauge, temperature, length, and current.

What happens if the PoE budget is exceeded?

Depending on the switch, new devices may fail to power up, lower-priority ports may be shut down, or the system may become unstable under peak load.
